A 64-year-old man presents to the emergency department with cough and shortness of breath that has progressively been worsening over the last several weeks. He has also been more fatigued and is unable to get around the house anymore. He denies fever and night sweats. He has a past medical history of hypertension and takes lisinopril daily. He is an immigrant from Argentina where he used to work as a miner. He denies tobacco use. What is the most likely diagnosis based on this chest radiograph?

  • AHistoplasmosis
  • BMiliary tuberculosis
  • CPneumoconiosis
  • DSarcoidosis










































Correct Answer ( C )

Explanation:

This radiograph demonstrates bilateral diffuse ground-glass opacities. Many diseases are capable of mimicking this radiograph appearance and include mycobacterial and fungal infections, malignancy, occupational lung diseases, and autoimmune lung diseases. Other risk factors need to be taken into consideration when determining the most likely diagnosis so that treatment can begin appropriately. Based on the information given, this patient is most likely presenting with pneumoconiosis, an occupational restrictive lung disease caused by inhalation of dust, often in mines, and from agriculture and construction work. Pneumoconiosis encompasses many occupational lung diseases including coal-workers’ pneumoconiosis, silicosis, and asbestosis to name a few. As a miner, this patient is at risk for pneumoconiosis. Diagnosis is based on risk factors, computed tomography imaging, biopsy, bronchoscopy, and failure to improve after treatment for infectious causes. Treatment is largely supportive and includes reducing further lung insults by limiting exposures. Corticosteroids have been used to decrease inflammation but no large randomized trials have been performed to truly know their benefit. The best way to avoid occupational lung disease is by prevention. Occupational Safety and Health Administration laws have been enacted in the United States since the 1970s to help prevent these illnesses but these regulations are not necessarily followed in other countries.

 

 

Histoplasmosis (A) is a fungal lung disease that can also present with these radiograph findings. However, patients at risk for histoplasmosis include the very old and very young, immunocompromised individuals, and people who live in areas where histoplasmosis is found in the soil. These areas of the United States generally include the Ohio and Mississippi River valleys. Miliary tuberculosis (B) is generally found in those with risk factors for having tuberculosis including immunocompromised individuals, those with previous tuberculosis, and individuals from endemic areas. Argentina is not one of those areas. Additionally, patients with long-standing tuberculosis more often present with low-grade fever and night sweats. Sarcoidosis (D) can affect any person but is more common in African-Americans and people of Scandinavian descent. Presentation is usually between the ages of 20 and 40 years. Although it can be difficult to differentiate sarcoidosis from other entities, the mining risk factor leads the clinician toward pneumoconiosis instead of sarcoidosis in this patient.

Question: What treatment for possible histoplasmosis should be initiated while awaiting confirmatory studies?

Reveal Answer: Amphotericin B.

Opioid withdrawal in the emergency setting

Four to eight mg of sublingual buprenorphine can be administered for acute opioid withdrawal. If symptoms persist 30 to 60 minutes later, a second dose up to 24 mg may be administered. Alternatively, 0.3 to 0.9 mg of intravenous (IV; given over 20 to 30 minutes) or intramuscular buprenorphine may be administered. Like methadone, buprenorphine is used as substitution therapy to treat opioid dependence. It can be given daily, or several times a week to patients as part of maintenance therapy. Buprenorphine is formulated alone for IV, transdermal, and oral administration and as an oral formulation combined with naloxone to discourage crushing and IV administration. Buprenorphine is a partial agonist and therefore a theoretical ceiling exists for its potential to cause respiratory depression. This feature, combined with its long duration of action and high affinity for opioid receptors, is advantageous in the long-term management of opioid dependence. Emergency department treatment initiation of buprenorphine may also increase the chance for future engagement in addiction treatment. Stolbach A, Hoffman RS.
