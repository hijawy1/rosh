A 57-year-old woman presents to the emergency department complaining of high blood pressure. She has been working on a big project at work and developed a slight bitemporal headache today. She decided to check her blood pressure and found it to be 188/116 mm Hg. Her colleagues encouraged her to be further evaluated so she came to the emergency department. She denies any other past medical history and does not take any medications. She denies any other symptoms aside from the mild headache which has now improved. In the emergency department, vital signs show HR 76 bpm, BP 178/104 mm Hg, R 12/min, T 98.6°F. Which of the following diagnostic tests would be most useful in managing this patient?

  • AChest radiograph
  • BComputed tomography of the head
  • CElectrocardiogram
  • DSerum creatinine










































Correct Answer ( D )

Explanation:

The patient in this question is presenting with asymptomatic hypertension. Current emergency medicine literature does not give definitive advice about which patients with asymptomatic elevated blood pressure should receive screening tests. Commonly ordered tests include a basic metabolic panel or creatinine to screen for renal dysfunction, electrocardiogram to evaluate for left ventricular hypertrophy or cardiac ischemia, chest radiograph for cardiomegaly or pulmonary edema, and urinalysis for proteinuria. However, clinically meaningful results exist in very few of those screened. To date, the only screening test that has shown some utility in changing management of patients with asymptomatic elevated blood pressure is a serum creatinine for renal dysfunction. No other diagnostic screening tests appear to be useful.

 

Chest radiograph (A) can help evaluate for cardiomegaly or pulmonary edema. However, in a patient with asymptomatic elevated blood pressure, chest radiograph has not been shown to change management. Although the patient in this question complained of a bitemporal headache when her blood pressure was elevated, she is not presenting with signs and symptoms characteristic of a hypertensive emergency. Elevated blood pressure, headache and focal neurologic deficits are associated with the hypertensive emergencies of ischemic and hemorrhagic strokes. Computed tomography of the head (B) would be indicated in such a scenario but not in this patient whose headache is improved and is not associated with neurologic complaints. Similar to the chest radiograph, an electrocardiogram (C) can help evaluate for left ventricular hypertrophy or cardiac ischemia, but in a patient without chest pain or cardiac symptoms there is little utility in this diagnostic test.

Question: If the patient in this question was presenting with a headache and focal neurologic deficits consistent with an ischemic stroke and tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) was indicated, what would be the target blood pressure once tPA was given?

Reveal Answer: Blood pressure less than 180/105 mm Hg.

Asymptomatic Hypertension

  • Most common causes of BP elevation: pain, anxiety 
  • No workup usually indicated, ED may consider checking Cr
  • Outpatient follow-up 

Newer recommendations indicate that the fourth or fifth intercostal space laterally or the second or third intercostal space anteriorly can be used as the site of needle decompression.
