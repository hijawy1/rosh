A 32-year-old woman with a history of hypothyroidism presents with chest pain. She reports it started suddenly about 2 hours ago and points to her midsternum when referring to the pain. The above chest X-ray is obtained. Which one of the following additional pieces of history would most likely help you make the diagnosis?

  • AChest pain worse with exertion
  • BFrequent NSAID use
  • CRecent forceful vomiting
  • DRecent viral upper respiratory infection
  • ERecurrent postprandial pain










































Correct Answer ( C )

Explanation:

The X-ray image above displays pneumomediastinum, or air in the mediastinum. There are many ways this can occur, including iatrogenic injury, blunt or penetrating trauma, increases in intraesophageal pressure as in a Valsalva maneuver or childbirth, and obstructive lung disorders. Of the answer choices available here, a reasonable cause could be Boerhaave syndrome, which is an esophageal perforation due to increased intraesophageal pressure when a person is vomiting or straining. Patients typically present with severe retrosternal chest pain and may have chest wall crepitus or a crackling sound with each heartbeat (Hamman sign) on physical exam. If diagnosis and treatment are delayed, patients will develop dyspnea and sepsis within hours. Boerhaave syndrome is often initially diagnosed on radiography, but small tears may be missed. If clinical suspicion is high, the patient should undergo an esophagram with water-soluble contrast, but if negative with high suspicion, a barium contrast esophagram is more sensitive. CT can be considered in those who cannot complete esophagography. Patients with a diagnosis of Boerhaave syndrome should be started on broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, made NPO, and consulted for early surgical intervention.



Chest pain that is worse with exertion (A) is concerning for acute coronary syndrome, which would not present with pneumomediastinum. Frequent NSAID use (B) is a risk factor for developing peptic ulcer disease (PUD) and gastritis. While PUD can erode through the intestinal wall, causing pneumoperitoneum, it would not cause pneumomediastinum. A recent viral infection (D) may indicate viral pericarditis. In this case, the heart may be enlarged secondary to a pericardial effusion, but this should not cause free air in the mediastinum. Recurrent postprandial pain (E) may represent cholelithiasis, gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), PUD, or gastritis, none of which would have pneumomediastinum on chest X-ray.

Question: True or false: less than half of all patients presenting with Boerhaave syndrome have a history of recent vomiting.

Reveal Answer: True, only 25–45% have a history of recent vomiting.

Pneumomediastinum

  • Spontaneous, trauma, Valsalva (crack users), Boerhaave syndrome (Hx of vomiting)
  • PE: crepitus, Hamman sign
  • Chest X-ray: ring around the right pulmonary artery
  • Usually self-resolving

Newer recommendations indicate that the fourth or fifth intercostal space laterally or the second or third intercostal space anteriorly can be used as the site of needle decompression.
