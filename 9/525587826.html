A 28-year-old man presents with his friends to the ED with altered mental status. The patient has no known medical or allergic history. His friends state that he was well up until one hour ago. They all have been camping, “living off the land,” and eating an assortment of berries and plants. His vital signs are remarkable for a temperature 39°C, HR 130, RR 26, BP 110/70, and 99% oxygen saturation on room air. Physical examination reveals an agitated, altered man with 9 mm pupils that are nonreactive to light. His oral mucosa is dry, he feels warm to the touch, and chest exam is remarkable for tachycardia and tachypnea. The axillary and inguinal folds are dry. No clonus or muscle rigidity is noted on exam. What is the likely cause of the patient’s presentation?

  • ACocaine ingestion
  • BFoxglove ingestion
  • CJimson weed ingestion
  • DSt. John’s wort ingestion










































Correct Answer ( C )

Explanation:

Jimson weed is a plant that can be unintentionally ingested, ingested for natural remedies, or ingested intentionally for the hallucinatory effects. Jimson weed (Datura stramonium) contains high concentrations of belladonna alkaloids (hyoscyamine, hyoscine, atropine, and scopolamine) and because of these substances it can produce anticholinergic toxicity. Anticholinergic toxicity is a frequently encountered occurrence in the ED and can be secondary to several substances in plants, drugs, and antispasmodics. Clinical features of anticholinergic toxicity is well known by “red as a beet, dry as a bone, hot as a hare, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter, and full as a flask.” These features correspond to erythema of the skin due to vasodilation, anhidrosis and dry mucous membranes, anhidrotic hyperthermia, nonreactive mydriasis, delirium, and urinary retention, respectively. Tachycardia and decreased or absent bowel sounds are clinical features that may also be present. Laboratory evaluation for anticholinergic agents is unhelpful and not readily available and therefore the diagnosis is made clinically. An ECG should be performed to evaluate for QRS and QTc intervals. Fingerstick glucose, acetaminophen and salicylate levels, and a pregnancy test should be obtained as well. Treatment initially is focused on stabilization of the patient’s ABCs. All patients should have intravenous access, cardiac monitoring, supplemental oxygen as needed, and continuous pulse oximetry. Sodium bicarbonate is reserved for patients with prolonged QRS intervals or dysrhythmias related to anticholinergic toxicity. Benzodiazepines can be used for agitation and seizures and are the preferred medications due to the lack of anticholinergic properties that phenothiazines and butyrophenones possess. Physostigmine therapy is reserved for patients with both peripheral and moderate central anticholinergic toxicity, which is defined as having severe agitation or delirium. Physostigmine should not be given if other conditions are suspected such as a tricyclic antidepressant overdose.

 

 

Cocaine ingestion (A) causes a sympathomimetic toxidrome that clinically appears similar to anticholinergic toxicity. Cocaine works at the synaptic cleft inhibiting the reuptake of norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin. The clinical features of cocaine ingestion or overdose are tachycardia, mydriasis, delirium, and hyperthermia. However, cocaine toxicity is associated with diaphoresis which is not present and is a contradistinction from anticholinergic toxicity. Foxglove ingestion (B) and overdose is similar in presentation to a digitalis overdose. This is because the foxglove plant, also known as Digitalis purpurea, has cardiac glycosides within the plant and is the first plant that was used for the treatment of heart failure. Other plants with similar properties include dogbane, red squill, lily of the valley, oleander, and henbane. Clinical features include nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, weakness, delirium, disorientation, and visual changes. The greatest concerns, however, are the cardiac effects seen with toxicity. Digitalis can produce a range of cardiac dysrhythmias ranging from premature ventricular contractions (PVCs), which are the most common rhythm disturbance, to ventricular dysrhythmias. Bradycardia, AV blocks, junctional rhythms, ventricular tachycardia, and ventricular fibrillation can also be seen. Bidirectional ventricular tachycardia is a rare but almost pathognomonic dysrhythmia that can be seen in digitalis toxicity. Digoxin levels, electrolytes, creatinine and BUN, and series ECGs should be performed. Hyperkalemia is an important marker for acute digitalis toxicity as it correlates with mortality. Kidney dysfunction has been shown to occur with digitalis toxicity and it will increase digitalis concentrations, making digitalis toxicity worse. The diagnosis is made clinically based on history, physical examination, and ECG manifestations as digoxin levels will not always correlate consistently with clinical manifestations. St. John’s wort ingestion (D) alone is unlikely to cause the clinical manifestations seen above. Clinical features suggestive of serotonin syndrome are more consistent with co-ingestion of St. John’s wort with an antidepressant. As with sympathomimetic toxicity, serotonin syndrome can present similarly to anticholinergic toxicity. Clinical features include agitation, delirium, tachycardia, hyperthermia, and hypertension. However, patients will also demonstrate diaphoresis, muscle rigidity, and clonus. Diaphoresis, as with sympathomimetic toxicity, is not seen in anticholinergic toxicity. Clonus helps differentiate sympathomimetic toxicity from serotonin syndrome because clonus is only seen in serotonin syndrome.

Question: Which clinical feature is the earliest and most reliable sign of anticholinergic toxicity?

Reveal Answer: Tachycardia.

Anticholinergic Ingestion

  • Antipsychotics, antihistamines, jimson weed, deadly nightshade
  • Mad as a hatter, red as a beet, dry as a bone, blind as a bat, hot as a hare
  • Physostigmine, BZDs, cooling

AHA 2015 Guideline update: The combined use of vasopressin and epinephrine offers no advantage to using standard-dose epinephrine in cardiac arrest. Also, vasopressin does not offer an advantage over the use of epinephrine alone. Therefore, to simplify the algorithm, vasopressin has been removed from the Adult Cardiac Arrest Algorithm-2015 Update.
