A 42-year-old man with alcohol use disorder presents with vomiting. His labs are notable for a metabolic acidosis with a normal glucose. His lactic acid is normal. Which of the following is a critical component of his treatment?

  • AFolic acid
  • BGlucose
  • CInsulin
  • DLactated ringers










































Correct Answer ( B )

Explanation:

Alcoholic ketoacidosis most commonly occurs in alcoholic patients. Patients binge drink with resultant vomiting and decreased food and liquid ingestion leading to dehydration. As they develop ketoacidosis, patients experience abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Patients develop ketonuria without significant hyperglycemia or glucosuria. The pH of the blood is relatively normal because of a concomitant metabolic alkalosis. The exact mechanism by which ketones are produced is unknown, although chronic malnutrition and acute starvation both contribute. Patients require a nutritional source and need glucose or dextrose administration. Given the presence of dehydration, fluid replacement is necessary. The metabolic alkalosis associated with this is frequently accompanied by hypokalemia which needs repletion. These patients should receive thiamine to prevent the development of Wernicke-Korsakoff syndrome.

 

 

Folic acid (A) is often administered to chronic alcoholic patients as part of nutritional supplementation because of poor dietary input and the inability of cirrhotic livers to store adequate folate reservoirs. However, its administration has no acute role in the management of alcoholic ketoacidosis. Insulin (C) is not part of the management of alcoholic ketoacidosis, but is an important piece of the treatment of diabetic ketoacidosis where the absence of insulin has led to fatty acid oxidation and the accumulation of serum ketones. In these patients, supplying continuous exposure to insulin (usually as a continuous intravenous infusion) will help stop fatty acid oxidation. Lactated Ringers (D) is a common source of fluid resuscitation often used in the resuscitation of trauma patients. It contains more physiologic components than normal saline, one of which is bicarbonate (in the form of lactate that is converted to bicarbonate in the body). It also contains 4 mEq of potassium which may help in this situation. However, most patients with alcoholic ketoacidosis have been vomiting and therefore have significant chloride losses. Sodium chloride is a better fluid choice in these patients because of its higher concentration of chloride (155 mEq/L versus 109 mEq/L).

Question: How long does it take to reverse alcoholic ketoacidosis?

Reveal Answer: 12–24 hours.
